PREPARATION OF BIOPLASTICS

Bioplastics are plastics derived from renewable biomass sources, such as plants, rather than from petroleum-based sources. There are various types of bioplastics, but the most common ones include biodegradable plastics (like PLA - polylactic acid and PHA - polyhydroxyalkanoates) and non-biodegradable bioplastics (such as bio-based polyethylene). Here's a basic outline of the general process for preparing bioplastics:

Materials needed :

  1. Starch (e.g., from corn, potato, or tapioca)
  2. distilled water
  3. glycerol(plasticizer)
  4. vinegar(acetic acid etc)
  5. Heat source
  6. Non stick surface

Procedure:

Step 1:

1. Raw Materials The raw materials for bioplastics can come from:
Starch: Derived from corn, potatoes, or tapioca. Polylactic acid (PLA): Made from fermented plant sugars, typically corn starch or sugarcane. Polyhydroxyalkanoates (PHA): Produced by bacteria that convert plant sugars or lipids into biodegradable plastic.

Cellulose: Obtained from wood or plant fibers.

Step 2:

2. Extraction and Processing Starch-based bioplastics: Starch is extracted from plant sources, then heated and mixed with plasticizers (like glycerol or sorbitol) to make it malleable. The resulting mixture is formed into sheets or films.

PLA production: First, lactic acid is produced by fermenting sugars from corn or other sources. Lactic acid undergoes polymerization to form PLA, using a catalyst.
PHA production: Bacteria are used to ferment plant sugars or lipids. The bacteria store the sugars as PHA, which is extracted and purified after fermentation.

Step 3:

3. Polymerization Polymerization is the process where monomers (the basic building blocks, like lactic acid or glycerol) are chemically bonded to form long polymer chains. For PLA, the monomer is lactic acid, and the polymerization can be carried out using ring-opening polymerization (ROP) or condensation polymerization. PHA is produced by bacterial fermentation, followed by the extraction of the polymer.

Step 4:

4. Forming the Bioplastic Once the polymer is created, it can be processed in various forms, including: Injection molding: The bioplastic is melted and injected into molds to create specific shapes. Extrusion: The bioplastic is melted and forced through a mold to create films, sheets, or fibers. Casting: Liquefied bioplastics can be poured into molds and allowed to solidify.

Step 5:

5. Additives Bioplastics may be modified with additives to enhance their properties: Plasticizers: To improve flexibility. Stabilizers: To prevent degradation from UV light or heat. Fillers: To increase strength or lower production costs (e.g., cellulose fibers). Colorants: For aesthetic purposes.

Step 6:

6. Final Product The final bioplastic product can be used in packaging, agricultural films, disposable items, or as a replacement for traditional plastic in various industries, depending on its properties.

Types of Bioplastics:


PLA (Polylactic Acid): Common in food packaging, biodegradable, derived from corn starch or sugarcane.

PHA (Polyhydroxyalkanoates): Biodegradable, produced by bacterial fermentation. PBS (Polybutylene Succinate): Biodegradable and often used in packaging, produced from plant-based sources.

Bio-based PET: Made from renewable resources but not biodegradable. Bioplastics are considered more sustainable compared to conventional plastics because they are made from renewable resources and may break down naturally, reducing plastic pollution. However, their production still has environmental impacts, so the type and scale of their production should be considered for environmental benefits.
